Risk Assessment of Digitalisation in Traditional Enterprises

The article by Kovaitė and Stankevičienė (2019), titled Risks of Digitalisation of Business Models, addresses the risks inherent in the digitalisation of business models within the context of Industry 4.0. This is presented as a revolution rapidly transforming business models through the integration of technologies such as the Internet of Things (IoT), Artificial Intelligence (AI), Big Data, and Cloud Computing. While these technologies accelerate organisational transformations, they also introduce specific new risks related to the digitalisation of business models.

Types of Identified Risks

  • Technical Risks: Relating to acquiring new skills and resources necessary for integrating technologies like IoT or Cloud Computing.
  • Competency Risks: Linked to the lack of skills among employees and the rapid adaptation to digitalisation processes.
  • Behavioural Risks: Involving user behaviours regarding digital solutions, particularly risks of excessive or inappropriate use.
  • Data Security Risks: Pertaining to data loss, cyberattacks, and security breaches.
  • Financial Risks: Significant investments and managing hidden costs associated with new technologies.

Risk Research and Assessment Methodology (FARE Method)

The authors use the FARE (Factor Relationship) method, a multi-criteria decision support approach. It helps evaluate the impact of various risks on the blocks of Osterwalder’s Business Model Canvas, including key partners, key activities, key resources, value proposition, customer relationships, distribution channels, customer segments, revenue streams, and cost structure. The expert evaluations are consolidated into a RADi (Risk Assessment of Digitalisation) matrix, which identifies the business model elements most affected by digitalisation risks.

Key Risk Assessment Results

The results indicate that distribution channels, key resources, and customer segments are the most vulnerable to digitalisation risks, while key partners are less affected. Competency and data security risks have a more significant impact than others on specific business model blocks.

Practical Recommendations

The authors suggest using the RADi matrix to support decision-making during the digitalisation of business models, helping anticipate risks and develop appropriate mitigation measures. The FARE method also allows for prioritising corrective actions based on the relationships between risk criteria.

Implications for Research and Policy

The article points out gaps in the literature concerning the macro-level risk assessment of digitalisation. It recommends future research to expand the RADi matrix and consider the social implications of Industry 5.0, which includes the societal effects of technological disruptions.
