The Future of SRM: Resilience to Emerging Threats

Objective: To demonstrate why resilience will be the most influential trend in security risk management (SRM) over the next five years, focusing on service continuity and protection against inevitable and sophisticated threats.

Context of Emerging Threats

AI and IoT

Threats related to AI include the manipulation of learning data and adversarial attacks, where AI models can be deceived. Autonomous systems such as self-driving cars are vulnerable to hacking, which could lead to serious accidents.

Increased Complexity and New Vulnerabilities

Modern information systems are increasingly complex, making it harder to secure each component. The expansion of IoT multiplies entry points for cyberattacks, with the growing number of connected devices (sensors, smart objects) posing challenges for uniform security standards.

Why Resilience is Key

Resilience is the ability of a system to continue functioning and providing its essential services, even when some of its components are compromised or attacked. This includes fault tolerance, redundancy, and quick recovery from attacks.

Growing Importance

It has become unrealistic to believe that all cyberattacks can be prevented. The challenge is ensuring continuity of service even when an attack is successful. Critical infrastructures, such as electrical grids and healthcare systems, require resilience to avoid catastrophic consequences.

Concrete Example

During the WannaCry ransomware attack, some critical infrastructures, such as hospitals, were able to continue functioning thanks to redundant systems and business continuity plans. This kind of preparation is essential in an environment where cyberattacks are becoming increasingly sophisticated.

Frameworks and Strategies to Strengthen Resilience

Recommended Approaches

  • Scalability and composability: Secure systems must scale without constant reevaluation of each component.
  • Diversification of components: Using different software or hardware platforms ensures that even if part of the system fails, others will continue to operate.
  • Predictive modeling: Models based on security metrics allow better prediction of vulnerabilities and anticipation of attacks.

Existing Frameworks

  • NIST Framework: Particularly suited for critical infrastructures with recommendations on system resilience.
  • ISO/IEC 27005: Manages risks while integrating business continuity strategies, ensuring essential services are maintained during incidents.

Legal, Social, and Ethical Issues

Compliance and regulation are essential in resilience strategies. Organizations must comply with regulations such as GDPR, while ethical responsibility arises when AI decisions lead to harmful outcomes, such as accidents caused by autonomous systems.

Conclusion and Call to Action

Summary: Resilience will be the most influential trend in SRM over the next five years. Organizations must invest now to ensure their critical systems can withstand increasingly sophisticated cyberattacks.

Call to Action: Governments and organizations should invest in research and technologies to guarantee better resilience and protect critical infrastructures long-term.

  • 7. In which industries is resilience most critical? Critical infrastructures (energy, healthcare, transport) and financial services will need the most resilience in the coming years.
