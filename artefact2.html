Industry 4.0: Innovations and Risks in the Modern World

Industry 4.0 refers to a new phase of the industrial revolution that integrates advanced digital technologies such as the Internet of Things (IoT), artificial intelligence (AI), and robotics into manufacturing and business processes, creating a smart and highly automated environment.

Examples of Industry 4.0

  • Additive Manufacturing (3D Printing): 3D printing revolutionizes the production of complex parts. For instance, General Electric uses it to create components for aircraft turbines, reducing time and costs (Gao et al., 2015).
  • Digital Twins: Digital twins offer real-time simulation and optimization of production processes. Siemens uses them to improve factory operations, reducing downtime (Tao et al., 2018).

Concrete Examples of Risks

  • Predictive Maintenance Risk: Misinterpreted data or errors in algorithms can lead to unplanned downtimes. For instance, a tool manufacturing company may face interruptions if maintenance predictions are incorrect (Lee et al., 2014).
  • Data Management Risk: Inadequate management of data in digital systems can cause compliance issues. A pharmaceutical company might face challenges ensuring regulatory compliance if sensor data is improperly stored (Sivarajah et al., 2017).

Review of Another Article

Another article by Kiel, Arnold, and Voigt (2017) supports the points raised by Kovaitė and Stankevičienė but highlights the specific challenges SMEs face in adopting Industry 4.0. They recommend strategic partnerships and training initiatives to help SMEs overcome financial and technical skill barriers.

Conclusion

While Industry 4.0 offers significant opportunities for innovation and efficiency, it also introduces substantial risks that need to be carefully managed. Risk assessment frameworks, such as those proposed by Kovaitė and Stankevičienė, are essential to help businesses safely navigate this digital transformation.
