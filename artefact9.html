Quantitative Risk Modelling

According to Goerlandt et al (2017), Hugo et al (2018) and Çelikbilek & Tüysüz (2020)

Part A: Academic Article Analysis and Responses

How do Goerlandt et al. (2017) validate QRA approaches?

Answer: Goerlandt et al. (2017) propose validating Quantitative Risk Assessment (QRA) models by comparing them with empirical data from real-life events. An iterative approach allows for continuous refinement.

Example: In a maritime transport company, if an accident occurs, the incident data would be used to adjust and validate the QRA model to improve its accuracy.

Which techniques did Hugo et al. (2018) propose for project management?

Answer: Hugo et al. (2018) recommend using probabilistic models, such as Monte Carlo simulations, from the project planning phase. They suggest integrating QRAs into project management tools and training project managers in these methods.

Example: A construction company could use QRAs to predict budget overruns by modeling factors like weather or material delays, allowing better risk management.

Findings of Çelikbilek & Tüysüz (2020) on MCDMs

Answer: They found TOPSIS to be highly accurate, but noted weaknesses like subjective weighting of criteria, which can lead to biases, and sensitivity to those weights.

Example: In supplier selection for a manufacturing company, if too much weight is given to "cost," it might result in selecting unreliable suppliers, thus TOPSIS should be calibrated carefully.

Part B: Monte Carlo Simulation

The Monte Carlo simulation models an inventory scenario over 30 days, with an average daily demand of 50 units (SD of 10 units). The goal is to assess the impact of demand variability on stock levels.

Assumptions:

  • Initial stock: 1000 units
  • Average daily demand: 50 units
  • Demand standard deviation: 10 units
  • Simulation duration: 30 days

Simulation Results

Final Inventory: After 30 days, the remaining stock was 443.56 units.

Stockouts: The company faced stockouts on 10 days due to demand variability.

Analysis and Interpretation

The simulation shows that stockouts occurred, highlighting the need for adjustments in inventory management to avoid shortages during periods of high demand.

Recommendations

  • Increase safety stock to prevent stockouts.
  • Implement real-time inventory tracking for better demand anticipation.
  • Reevaluate demand forecasts to better manage fluctuations.

Conclusion

This seminar demonstrated the importance of refining quantitative risk models using real-world data and showed how Monte Carlo simulations can improve risk management in inventory and project planning. Companies should apply these tools to better predict and mitigate risks.
