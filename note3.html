Peer feedback

This page highlights the peer contributions to my work throughout the Collaborative disussion 2 about "Analysis of CVSS: Characteristics, critique and Alternatives", where they provided constructive feedback to improve the quality and depth of the topic.

Peer Response 1

Hi Irina,

Thank you for sharing your thoughts about CVSS.

While the latest version of CVSS was recently published and offers significant improvements over its predecessor, the system still fails to address important aspects such as inadequate representation of real-world risk and limited scope and lack of context, which includes the potential for chained exploits (Divinsky, 2023).

I would argue that the chaining of different vulnerabilities is an easy-to-understand topic, and neither is the modeling of such threats. Robinson (2022) concluded in her paper titled “An Exploratory Study into Vulnerability Chaining Blindness Terminology and Viability” that IT and cybersecurity professionals often lack awareness of vulnerability chaining, a term that refers to the potential compounding effects of multiple vulnerabilities. Although cybersecurity professionals acknowledge its importance, the term is not well-understood or widely used across the board, especially within IT operations teams.

There is a gap between IT and cybersecurity professionals. While both groups recognize the need for holistic, risk-based management of security, IT professionals are more concerned with system functionality and user needs, rather than focusing on the broader implications of lower-priority vulnerabilities that could be exploited in combination. On the other hand, cybersecurity professionals see the value in addressing these vulnerabilities but struggle with the complexity and sheer volume of information required to implement defense in depth effectively.

Peer Response 2

Thank you Irina for sharing your thoughts.

The criticisms of CVSS by Spring et al. (2021) highlight significant shortcomings that undermine its effectiveness in assessing vulnerabilities. One major issue is the lack of empirical justification for its calculation formula, which raises questions about the reliability of the scores generated. In sensitive sectors such as healthcare, where system availability can be crucial, an incorrect assessment could have disastrous consequences (Chase and Coley, 2019). Equally weighting confidentiality, integrity, and availability axes does not take into account sector specificities. For example, a vulnerability that threatens availability can put lives at risk.

Furthermore, CVSS assesses vulnerabilities in isolation, ignoring the risks associated with complex attacks that exploit multiple weaknesses in a chain. This approach is particularly problematic in interconnected systems, where vulnerabilities can interact in a non-linear manner, leading to overall impacts that are often underestimated (Farris et al., 2018).

On the other hand, cyber risk quantification (CRQ) appears to be a more suitable method. It allows organizations to assess risks in financial terms, thus providing a better understanding of the economic impacts of each vulnerability. This contextualized approach, oriented towards the specific consequences of each sector, facilitates more informed strategic decision-making.

In short, it is imperative to adopt assessment methodologies that consider the operational context to improve risk management in critical environments, where precision is essential.
