Applying Threat Modeling and Management Techniques

Focus on the practical application of threat modeling and management techniques, relying on essential resources like the Threat Modeling Manifesto, the OWASP Cookbook, and the MITRE ATT&CK database. The objective was to apply the methods previously studied and create threat models for real-world scenarios.

What I Learned

  • Tools and resources for threat modeling: It allowed me to explore key resources like the Threat Modeling Manifesto, which advocates for a collaborative approach, and the MITRE ATT&CK database, which catalogs concrete techniques used by attackers. I learned how these resources provide tools for modeling threats in various contexts.
  • Using publicly available tools for threat modeling: We evaluated the effectiveness of several tools, such as STRIDE, DREAD, and Attack Trees, as well as threat libraries, to identify and mitigate risks across different systems. These tools help structure threat analysis and adapt mitigation strategies accordingly.
  • Creating threat models for real-world scenarios: The unit emphasized the application of these techniques to scenarios like securing an airport or a nuclear power plant. This gave me a deeper understanding of how to apply these methods in real and complex environments.

Learning Outcomes

  • Knowledge of key tools and resources: At the end, I gained an in-depth knowledge of critical resources like the Threat Modeling Manifesto, the OWASP Cookbook, and the MITRE ATT&CK database, which provide libraries, tools, and techniques for identifying and modeling threats.
  • Selecting the appropriate tool for various situations: I learned how to evaluate tools based on the scenario. For example, STRIDE is useful for complex systems, while DREAD helps rank threats based on their severity. It helped me understand when and how to use these tools effectively.
  • Critical evaluation of tool effectiveness: I was also able to assess the flexibility and structure of these tools in different contexts. While STRIDE is efficient for structuring threats, it can lack flexibility in dynamic environments, whereas Attack Trees are particularly effective for visualizing complex attack scenarios.

It allowed me to put into practice the concepts studied earlier, which was particularly rewarding. Modeling threats for real-world scenarios, such as an international airport or a nuclear power plant, gave me a better perspective on the applicability of different techniques.
